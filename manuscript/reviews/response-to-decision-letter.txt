Fredrik Ronquist

The paper presents TreeFlow, a python library for probabilistic modelling and
automatic differentiation with phylogenetic trees. Software that supports more
flexible probabilistic modelling approaches to statistical phylogenetics
represents an important and promising research direction, I believe. The paper
is well written, and I find it fairly easy to follow even though some parts are
quite technical. The empirical examples indicate some promise with respect to
the reliability of results from variational inference. Also, the analyses of
computational speed show that the increased modeling flexibility comes with an
overhead that is acceptable, especially if optimized software is not available
for the model of interest.

The one major concern I have is the brief description of the kappa-variation
model. It leaves a number of uncertainties concerning the structure of the model
and how to interpret the empirical results obtained from it. See detailed
comments below for Figure 3 in the manuscript. I would also suggest some
expansion of the description of the software, like how to install it, and where
to start if you are interested in learning how to use it. The remaining comments
and suggestions are minor and should be relatively easy for the authors to
address.

*** RESPONSE: *****************************************************************
We thank the reviewer for the positive assessment. We have substantially
expanded the description of the kappa-variation model, both in the main text and
in the caption of Figure 3, to make its structure and interpretation
unambiguous (see our responses to the Figure 3, L362 and L383-386 comments

L212-222: This paragraph is difficult to follow. Is it possible to provide a
more intuitive explanation for the quadratic cost?

*** RESPONSE: *****************************************************************
Yes. We have added an intuitive explanation: because a Tensor cannot be modified
in place, a traversal step that updates one node's value cannot overwrite that
entry but must produce a whole new copy of the tree-wide state. Repeating this
for each of the roughly N nodes creates N copies each of size N, and
differentiating through all of them, so both computation and memory grow with
the square of the number of nodes -- which is why the write-once TensorArray is
used instead.

L372-374. I think it would be advantageous to explain the step from importance
sampling to marginal likelihood estimates in one or two sentences, as this may
not be clear to all readers.

*** RESPONSE: *****************************************************************
Added. We now explain the step explicitly: one draws samples from the fitted
approximation and weights each by the ratio of the model's joint density (the
likelihood times the prior) to the density of the approximation; the average of
these importance weights is an unbiased Monte Carlo estimate of the marginal
likelihood, obtained directly rather than as the lower bound optimised during
variational inference.

Fig. 3. Text below the graph needs to be incorporated in the caption. I
understood kappa to be inferred per branch but this does not seem to be
consistent with the graphs. Does "lineage age" refer to the time duration
(length) of the branch? If so, please use "branch length" instead of "lineage
age", and use "Branch length" on the x axis. If lineage age does not refer to
branch length, then what does lineage age refer to? Please clarify. Is it that
"Age" refers to the median or ancestor or child age of the branch, and "Kappa"
to the kappa estimate for the branch? Or is the "Kappa" value some composite of
the different values of kappa from the node that represents the age to the tip
of the tree? Or have I completely misunderstood the model? Does it involve some
model of change of kappa over time or at speciation events, or is it a completely
branch-independent kappa values model, as I guessed?

*** RESPONSE: *****************************************************************
The reviewer's final guess is correct: it is a completely branch-independent
kappa model. Each branch is assigned its own independent kappa parameter, drawn
from a shared log-normal prior sampled once per branch; kappa is not shared
between branches and does not follow any model of change over time or at
speciation events. We have rewritten the Figure 3 captions to make this explicit
and to fold the previous sub-caption text into the main caption.

Regarding the axis: "Age" (formerly "lineage age") does NOT refer to branch
length. Because the tree is estimated in units of expected substitutions per
site and is not calibrated to time, "Age" is the height of the branch's lineage
above the present, measured in expected substitutions per site. The caption now
states this and explicitly notes that it is not the branch length. Each point is
the posterior median kappa for one branch plotted against that branch's lineage
age, with 95% credible intervals shown as vertical (kappa) and horizontal (age)
error bars. We considered relabelling the x-axis "Branch length" as suggested,
but did not, because that would be incorrect for this quantity; instead we have
defined "Age" precisely in the caption.

L383-386: "The growth in uncertainty in kappa estimates deeper in the tree..." I
think this sentence refers to age estimates and not kappa estimates (for which
the pattern is the opposite).

*** RESPONSE: *****************************************************************
The reviewer is correct, and we thank them for catching this. The growth in
uncertainty deeper in the tree is in the age (lineage) estimates, not the kappa
estimates, whose uncertainty is in fact largest for the youngest, shortest
branches. We have rewritten the passage accordingly: the uncertainty in the
lineage age estimates grows for branches deeper in the tree (consistent with
Duchene et al. 2015), while the uncertainty in the per-branch kappa estimates
shows the opposite pattern, being largest for the youngest, shortest branches,
which accumulate too few substitutions to constrain kappa.

L412-413. "The uncertainty..." The uncertainty appears to be significantly
underestimated for tree height and population size, but not for clock rate.
Please reconsider this summary of the results. Also, the uncertainty in the
stationary frequency of T seems to be significantly underestimated both in this
example and the previous example. Any intuition why this might be the case?

*** RESPONSE: *****************************************************************
Reconsidered and revised. The summary now states that the uncertainty is
substantially underestimated for the tree height and effective population size,
but only slightly for the clock rate. We have also added the observation that the
stationary frequency of T is noticeably underestimated in both examples, whereas
the other base frequencies are approximated well, and offered an intuition: the
base frequencies are constrained to sum to one and are represented in the
variational approximation through a transform in which the final coordinate is
determined by the others, so the mean-field family cannot capture the dependence
this induces and tends to compress the marginal variance of that dependent
coordinate.

L438-440. "Convergence..." I think users need more instructions on how to
properly monitor convergence of TreeFlow VI.

*** RESPONSE: *****************************************************************
Expanded. The text now describes concretely how convergence is monitored in
TreeFlow: the command line interface reports an ELBO estimate, and the full trace
of the loss and variational parameters can be saved (with --trace-output) and
plotted to confirm that the ELBO has reached a stable plateau and that the
variational parameters have stopped drifting, rerunning with more optimisation
steps if in doubt. We have also added a dedicated "Monitoring convergence of
variational inference" page to the online manual, to which the text now refers.

L440-444. It is difficult to assess the computational efficiency comparison
without more details. The conclusion that TreeFlow VI provides "substantial speed
improvements" does not seem to be consistent with the reported time durations,
unless the multiple preliminary runs in Beast took a substantial amount of time
(which might be the case for what I know). Perhaps some more detailed
justification of this conclusion?

*** RESPONSE: *****************************************************************
We have revised this conclusion. We agree that the single-run wall-clock times do
not by themselves support a claim of substantial speed improvement, and we have
removed that wording. The text now states that the single variational run is of
the same order as, and somewhat longer than, the reported BEAST 2 runtime, and
explains that the two figures are not directly comparable: the BEAST 2 time is
for the production run only and excludes the multiple preliminary runs that were
needed to tune operator weights and reach acceptable effective sample sizes,
whereas the variational analysis was run once and required no such manual tuning.
We now describe TreeFlow VI as a viable option whose optimisation-based approach
is expected to scale more favourably than random-walk MCMC as datasets grow,
rather than as being substantially faster in this example.
